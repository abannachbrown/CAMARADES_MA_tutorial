Welcome

In this web-based tutorial complements the workshop: “Introduction to Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis of Animal Studies”


We will use example data from the publication:

McCann, S.K., Cramond, F., Macleod, M.R. et al. Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of the Efficacy of Interleukin-1 Receptor Antagonist in Animal Models of Stroke: an Update. Transl. Stroke Res. 7, 395–406 (2016).

Background Information on the Dataset:
Interleukin-1 receptor antagonist (IL-1 RA) is an anti-inflammatory protein used clinically to treat rheumatoid arthritis and is considered a promising candidate therapy for stroke. In this systematic review and meta-analysis, we sought to assess efficacy in animal models of ischaemic stroke, the range of circumstances in which efficacy has been tested and whether the data appear to be confounded due to reported study quality and publication bias. The outcomes we assessed were post-stroke infarct volume (the amount of brain damage), neurobehavioural outcomes e.g. motor deficits, and mortality.

1. Introduction to Meta-Analysis

In the first section of this tutorial we will cover the basic steps of a meta-analysis: - Importing data - Calculating effect size - Conducting a random-effects meta-analysis

1.1.1 Effect sizes for infarct volume (NMD)

In this section of the tutorial we will calculate the effect sizes for our infarct volume data, which is a continuous variable. An effect size is a measure of the difference between two experimental cohorts. Here, we will calculate a “normalised mean difference” (NMD) using the control (vehicle-treated) and treatment (IL-1RA-treated) groups. The effect size is expressed as the proportional or percentage improvement in the treatment group. NMD is not widely used in clinical systematic reviews and there is no inbuilt calculation in the meta-analysis package we are using. Therefore, we will calculate NMD effect sizes manually.

)

2. Detecting small study effects

Small study effects in meta-analysis can be assessed using a funnel plot, which is a scatterplot of individual study effect sizes against a measure of each study’s precision. Ideally, the plot should approximately resemble a symmetrical (inverted) funnel. If there is bias due to missing results, for example because smaller studies without statistically significant effects remain unpublished, this will lead to an asymmetrical appearance of the funnel plot.
